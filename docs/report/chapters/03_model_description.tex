\chapter{Model Description}
\label{ch:model_description}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=immunecell!10, colframe=immunecell!50, title=\textbf{Into the Virtual Tumor}]
\textit{Welcome to the most detailed computational tumor ever built. In this chapter, we pull back the curtain on our virtual microenvironment—eighteen different cell types, each with its own agenda, navigating a 3D world of chemical gradients, spatial constraints, and life-or-death decisions.}
\end{tcolorbox}

Imagine shrinking down to the cellular scale and stepping into a kidney cancer. You'd find yourself in a bustling three-dimensional metropolis where every citizen has a specific job: tumor cells focused on growth and survival, immune cells hunting invaders, blood vessels delivering supplies, hormones carrying messages. This is the world our model creates—a virtual tumor microenvironment where individual cellular decisions aggregate into the complex dynamics that determine patient survival.

But building a convincing virtual tumor requires more than just digital cells bumping into each other. It requires capturing the intricate communication networks, the spatial constraints that shape interactions, the chemical gradients that guide cellular behavior, and the emergent phenomena that arise when hundreds of autonomous agents pursue their individual goals simultaneously.

This chapter takes you inside our computational creation, revealing the architecture that transforms biological knowledge into digital reality.

\section{The Architecture of Virtual Biology}
\label{sec:md_overview}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Building the Cellular Stage}
\label{subsec:md_spatial}

Every great drama needs a stage, and our cellular drama unfolds in a three-dimensional grid that captures the spatial reality of tumor tissue. This isn't an arbitrary computational convenience—it's a deliberate representation of biological architecture, where distance matters, neighborhoods shape destiny, and position determines survival.

\textbf{The Grid: A Cubic Universe}

Our virtual tissue is a cube with dimensions calculated directly from patient data:
\begin{equation}
    W = \left\lfloor \sqrt[3]{\frac{V_{\text{tumor}}}{V_{\text{block}}}} \right\rfloor
    \label{eq:grid_dim}
\end{equation}

where $V_{\text{tumor}}$ comes from the patient's medical imaging and $V_{\text{block}}$ represents how much real tissue each grid cell represents. This means a larger tumor gets a larger simulation space—the virtual world scales with the clinical reality it's trying to model.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=tumorcell!10, colframe=tumorcell!50, title=\textbf{Spatial Rules That Matter}]
\textbf{Sticky Borders:} Cells that try to move beyond the grid boundaries get "stuck" at the edge, creating natural accumulation zones that represent tissue margins. No cell disappears into computational limbo.

\textbf{Dense Packing:} Multiple cells can occupy the same grid position, just like in real tissue where cells squeeze together at incredible densities. A single grid position might host a tumor cell, two T cells, a blood vessel, and several hormone molecules.

\textbf{Hybrid Architecture:} The discrete cellular world is overlaid with a continuous glucose field—capturing both the digital reality of individual agents and the smooth chemical gradients that guide their behavior.
\end{tcolorbox}

This hybrid approach reflects a fundamental insight: biology operates simultaneously at discrete scales (individual cells making decisions) and continuous scales (chemical gradients diffusing through tissue). Our model captures both.

\textbf{The Biological Reality Behind the Numbers}

Each agent type represents years of biological research distilled into computational rules. Tumor cells (Agent 0) aren't just generic "bad cells"—they carry unique genetic profiles, respond to metabolic constraints, and evolve under selection pressure. Cytotoxic T cells (Agent 1) don't just "kill things"—they undergo activation, experience exhaustion, and respond to checkpoint inhibition.

The beauty of this multi-agent approach is that complex system behaviors emerge from the interaction of these simple agent rules. Immune escape doesn't have to be programmed—it emerges when tumor cells with the right mutations survive T cell attacks. Treatment resistance isn't coded as a feature—it develops when drug-resistant clones outcompete their neighbors.

\subsection{The Simulation Heartbeat: Orchestrating Cellular Chaos}
\label{subsec:md_timestep}

Every simulation step represents a moment in biological time—perhaps minutes or hours in the life of a tumor. But capturing that moment requires careful orchestration of hundreds or thousands of individual cellular decisions. Think of each time step as conducting a vast cellular orchestra, where every musician (agent) must play their part in perfect synchronization.

The \texttt{RCCModel.step()} method choreographs this complex dance through eleven precisely ordered operations:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=glucose!10, colframe=glucose!50, title=\textbf{The Simulation Sequence: Order Matters}]
\textbf{1. Collect Intelligence:} Before anything happens, record the current state—population counts, glucose levels, who killed whom. This becomes the historical record that tracks tumor evolution.

\textbf{2. Check for Victory or Defeat:} Evaluate whether the simulation should end. Has the tumor been eliminated? Has it grown beyond control? Are we out of time?

\textbf{3. Apply Chemical Communication:} Cells "sense" their neighborhoods, collecting effect signals from nearby agents. This is how immune cells detect inflammation, how tumor cells sense growth factors.

\textbf{4. Deliver Treatment:} If treatment has begun, apply drug effects. ICI removes immune brakes, TKI blocks angiogenesis, combination therapy does both.

\textbf{5. Sex-Dependent Immune Drift:} Apply hormonal influences on immune cell populations—testosterone suppresses, estrogen modulates.
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{6. Update the Chemical Landscape:} Blood vessels inject glucose, which diffuses through tissue while being consumed by hungry cells. The glucose field evolves according to physical laws.

\textbf{7. Manage the Blood Supply:} Evaluate whether new blood vessels should form, and update which tumor cells have access to nutrients.

\textbf{8. Adjust Detection Ranges:} As tumors grow, immune cells can detect them from further away—a larger threat casts a larger shadow.

\textbf{9. Agent Action Phase:} Every agent decides what to do—move, attack, divide, die, signal. But movement decisions are recorded, not executed, to avoid order effects.

\textbf{10. Mass Migration:} All movement happens simultaneously, preventing early-moving agents from unfairly claiming the best positions.

\textbf{11. Hormone Release:} Sex hormones are released from blood vessels into the tissue, carrying their molecular messages throughout the microenvironment.

\section{Agent Hierarchy}
\label{sec:md_hierarchy}
% ============================================================================

The agent class hierarchy reflects the biological taxonomy of the cell types and the shared functionality among related agent types.

\subsection{Base Cell Class}
\label{subsec:md_cell}

The \texttt{Cell} class (defined in \texttt{src/agents/cell.py}) is the abstract base class for all cellular agents. It inherits from both Python's \texttt{ABC} and \texttt{repast4py.core.Agent}, combining abstract method enforcement with Repast4Py's agent identity management.

Key attributes and methods of \texttt{Cell}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{pos}: The $(x, y, z)$ position tuple on the 3D grid.
    \item \texttt{size\_diameter}: The spatial extent of the cell (default 1).
    \item \texttt{\_desired\_pos}: The target position for deferred movement.
    \item \texttt{experienced\_effects}: An \texttt{Effect} object accumulating all effects received from neighbors.
    \item \texttt{search\_dimension}: The radius within which the agent searches for targets.
    \item \texttt{\_alive}: Boolean flag; once set to \texttt{False}, the agent is considered dead and pending removal.
    \item \texttt{base\_step()}: Common pre-step logic that applies a base death probability (\texttt{w\_cell\_base\_death\_prob}), removing the cell with a small random chance each step.
    \item \texttt{move(new\_pos)}: Sets \texttt{\_desired\_pos} without immediately moving the agent.
    \item \texttt{move\_towards(target\_type, look\_up\_size, distance)}: Finds the nearest agent of the specified type, computes a normalized direction vector, and sets a deferred move toward it.
    \item \texttt{find\_one(agent\_type\_id, radius)}: Returns a single agent of the given type within the specified Moore neighborhood radius.
    \item \texttt{collect\_effects()}: Gathers \texttt{Effect} objects from all neighboring \texttt{Cell} instances that \texttt{has\_effect()}.
    \item \texttt{duplicate()}: Creates a copy of the agent in an empty neighboring position.
    \item \texttt{sigmoid(x, k)}: Utility function returning $1 / (1 + e^{-kx})$, used for probability normalization.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{TCell Base Class}
\label{subsec:md_tcell}

The \texttt{TCell} class (defined in \texttt{src/agents/t\_cell.py}) extends \texttt{Cell} with functionality specific to T lymphocytes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hormone perception.} A dictionary \texttt{perceived\_sex\_hormone} tracks accumulated levels of estrogen, testosterone, progesterone, and androgen. The \texttt{perceive\_sex\_hormone()} method searches the neighborhood for sex hormone agents of the specified type, consumes them (removes from the model), and increments the perceived level. Hormone levels decay by 10\% per step via \texttt{apply\_hormonal\_decay()}.

    \item \textbf{TCR receptor matching.} Each T cell carries an 8-bit integer \texttt{receptor} value, generated randomly at creation. Antigen recognition is determined by the \texttt{is\_matching(antigen, receptor)} method, which computes the Hamming distance between the receptor and the SHA-256 hash of the antigen peptide sequence (truncated to 8 bits via \texttt{amino\_to\_8bit\_hash()}). A match occurs if:
    \begin{equation}
        d_H\bigl(\text{hash}(\text{antigen}),\; \text{receptor}\bigr) \leq 3 + \Delta_{\text{threshold}}
        \label{eq:hamming_match}
    \end{equation}
    where $d_H$ is the Hamming distance and $\Delta_{\text{threshold}}$ is the \texttt{receptor\_threshold\_variation} weight parameter. Additionally, the antigen must not be a self-antigen (i.e., not present in the wildtype protein sequences).

    \item \textbf{Sex hormone modulation.} The method \texttt{compute\_sexhormone\_modulation()} computes composite effects from perceived estrogen, testosterone, and progesterone levels. Each hormone has three regimes (low, medium, high, defined by normalized level thresholds at 0.33 and 0.66) that map to different effect profiles. The effects are weighted by hormone-specific weights (estrogen: 0.45, testosterone: 0.30, progesterone: 0.25) and accumulated in the \texttt{accumulated\_effects} dictionary, which spans 17 hormonal influence categories including kill rate, proliferation, apoptosis, activation, differentiation, Th1/Th2 proliferation, cytokine production, MHC regulation, costimulation, and checkpoint effects.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Concrete Agent Classes}
\label{subsec:md_concrete}

Each of the 18 agent types is implemented as a concrete class that inherits from either \texttt{Cell}, \texttt{TCell}, or \texttt{repast4py.core.Agent} directly:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Inheriting from \texttt{TCell}} (and thus \texttt{Cell}): \texttt{CytotoxicTCell}, \texttt{CD8NaiveTCell}, \texttt{CD4NaiveTCell}, \texttt{CD4Helper1TCell}, \texttt{CD4Helper2TCell}.
    \item \textbf{Inheriting from \texttt{Cell}} directly: \texttt{TumorCell}, \texttt{TregCell}, \texttt{DendriticCell}, \texttt{PlasmacitoidDendriticCell}, \texttt{MacrophageM1}, \texttt{MacrophageM2}, \texttt{NaturalKiller}, \texttt{MastCell}, \texttt{Neutrophil}, \texttt{Adipocyte}, \texttt{Blood}.
    \item \textbf{Inheriting from \texttt{repast4py.core.Agent}} directly: \texttt{SexHormone}, \texttt{Cytokine}. These non-cellular agents do not participate in the effect system and have simplified state (position, type-specific attributes, alive flag).
\end{itemize}

\section{Effect System}
\label{sec:md_effects}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Effect Fields}
\label{subsec:md_effect_fields}

The effect system is the primary mechanism for paracrine signaling between agents. Each \texttt{Cell} agent maintains an \texttt{experienced\_effects} object of type \texttt{Effect} (defined in \texttt{src/systems/effect.py}), which contains 14 floating-point fields representing the accumulated influence of neighboring cells:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{t\_kill\_rate\_effect} --- modulates cytotoxic T cell and NK cell kill probability.
    \item \texttt{t\_proliferation\_effect} --- modulates T cell proliferation rate.
    \item \texttt{t\_apoptosis\_effect} --- modulates T cell apoptosis probability.
    \item \texttt{t\_activation\_effect} --- modulates T cell activation level (affects kill rate and proliferation multiplicatively).
    \item \texttt{th1\_proliferation\_effect} --- specifically modulates Th1 cell proliferation.
    \item \texttt{th2\_proliferation\_effect} --- specifically modulates Th2 cell proliferation.
    \item \texttt{treg\_differentiation\_effect} --- governs CD4$^+$ naive $\to$ Treg differentiation probability.
    \item \texttt{macrophage\_m1\_mutation\_effect} --- governs M1 $\to$ M2 macrophage polarization probability.
    \item \texttt{macrophage\_m2\_mutation\_effect} --- governs M2 $\to$ M1 macrophage repolarization probability.
    \item \texttt{angiogenesis\_effect} --- governs the probability of new blood vessel formation.
    \item \texttt{tumour\_growth\_effect} --- modulates tumor cell duplication probability.
    \item \texttt{tumour\_apoptosis\_effect} --- modulates tumor cell apoptosis probability.
    \item \texttt{dc\_phagocitosis\_effect} --- modulates dendritic cell and pDC phagocytosis probability.
    \item \texttt{nkl\_kill\_rate\_effect} --- modulates NK cell kill probability.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{BMI Normalization}
\label{subsec:md_bmi}

Three of the 14 effect fields are initialized with BMI-dependent baseline values rather than zero:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{treg\_differentiation\_effect}: initialized to $-\hat{B} \times w_{\text{BMI\_on\_treg\_diff}}$
    \item \texttt{macrophage\_m1\_mutation\_effect}: initialized to $-\hat{B} \times w_{\text{BMI\_on\_m1\_mutation}}$
    \item \texttt{macrophage\_m2\_mutation\_effect}: initialized to $+\hat{B} \times w_{\text{BMI\_on\_m2\_mutation}}$
    \item \texttt{nkl\_kill\_rate\_effect}: initialized to $-\hat{B} \times w_{\text{BMI\_nkl\_kill\_rate}}$
\end{itemize}

\noindent where the normalized BMI is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{B} = \frac{\text{BMI} - \mu_{\text{BMI}}}{\sigma_{\text{BMI}}}
    \label{eq:bmi_norm}
\end{equation}
with sex-specific parameters $\mu_{\text{BMI}} = 26.5$, $\sigma_{\text{BMI}} = 5.5$ for females and $\mu_{\text{BMI}} = 27.5$, $\sigma_{\text{BMI}} = 4.5$ for males. This normalization ensures that a BMI above the population mean increases pro-tumorigenic effects (M2 polarization, Treg differentiation) and decreases anti-tumorigenic effects (NK killing, M1 stability), consistent with clinical observations linking obesity to poorer RCC outcomes.

\subsection{Effect Collection and Aggregation}
\label{subsec:md_effect_collection}

At each simulation step, before agents execute their individual behaviors, the model's \texttt{apply\_effects()} method iterates over all agents. For each agent that returns \texttt{True} from \texttt{needs\_effect()}, the \texttt{collect\_and\_apply\_effects()} method:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Queries the Moore neighborhood (radius 1) via the spatial index.
    \item For each neighbor that is a \texttt{Cell} instance and returns \texttt{True} from \texttt{has\_effect()}, retrieves its \texttt{Effect} object via \texttt{get\_effect()}.
    \item Aggregates all collected effects with the agent's current \texttt{experienced\_effects} via the \texttt{sum\_with()} method, which performs element-wise addition across all 14 fields.
\end{enumerate}

The \texttt{Effect.EFFECT\_RADIUS} class variable is set to 3, although the actual collection radius in the current implementation is 1 (Moore neighborhood).

\section{Movement}
\label{sec:md_movement}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Deferred Movement Pattern}
\label{subsec:md_deferred}

To prevent order-dependent artifacts---where an agent that moves early in the step occupies a position that a later agent expects to be empty---the model uses a deferred movement pattern:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item During their \texttt{step()} execution, agents call \texttt{self.move(new\_pos)}, which sets \texttt{self.\_desired\_pos = new\_pos} without modifying the spatial index.
    \item After all agents have stepped, the model's \texttt{move\_all\_agents()} method iterates over all living \texttt{Cell} agents and, for each agent whose \texttt{\_desired\_pos} differs from \texttt{pos}, updates the spatial index and grid position.
\end{enumerate}

This two-phase approach ensures that all agents make movement decisions based on the same spatial snapshot, and it eliminates bias arising from the random activation order.

\subsection{Directed Movement: \texttt{move\_towards()}}
\label{subsec:md_move_towards}

The primary movement mechanism for most agents is \texttt{move\_towards(target\_agent\_type\_id, look\_up\_size, distance)}, which:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Retrieves all living agents of the target type.
    \item Finds the closest target by Manhattan distance:
    \begin{equation}
        d_M(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = |a_x - b_x| + |a_y - b_y| + |a_z - b_z|
        \label{eq:manhattan}
    \end{equation}
    \item If the closest target is within \texttt{look\_up\_size}, computes the Euclidean direction vector $\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{p}_{\text{target}} - \mathbf{p}_{\text{self}}$, normalizes it, and scales by \texttt{distance}.
    \item Rounds each component and clamps to grid boundaries (sticky borders).
    \item Sets the resulting position as \texttt{\_desired\_pos}.
\end{enumerate}

The \texttt{look\_up\_size} parameter is typically derived from the model's \texttt{cell\_search\_dimension}, which grows with the tumor cell count to allow immune cells to detect tumors at greater distances as the tumor mass expands.

\subsection{Glucose Chemotaxis}
\label{subsec:md_chemotaxis}

When an immune cell (CTL, M1 macrophage, DC, or NK cell) fails to find a direct target via \texttt{move\_towards()}, it may fall back to glucose chemotaxis. With probability \texttt{w\_glucose\_chemotaxis\_strength}, the agent queries the glucose field for the discretized gradient at its position:

\begin{equation}
    (\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z) = \text{sign}\left(\nabla C_{\text{glucose}}(\mathbf{x})\right)
    \label{eq:chemotaxis_step}
\end{equation}

\noindent where the gradient is computed via central finite differences with Neumann (zero-flux) boundary conditions. The agent then sets its desired position to $\mathbf{x} + (\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z)$, clamped to grid bounds. This mechanism causes immune cells to migrate toward glucose-rich regions---typically areas near blood vessels and actively growing tumor masses---providing a biologically plausible navigation strategy in the absence of direct antigen detection.

\subsection{Sex Hormone Diffusion}
\label{subsec:md_hormone_diffusion}

Sex hormone agents follow a distinct movement model. At each step, a hormone agent randomly selects one of two diffusion strategies (each with 50\% probability):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Biased diffusion.} The hormone evaluates all 26 Moore neighbors, computes the Manhattan distance from each to the nearest blood vessel, and selects a target position via weighted random choice. The weighting biases movement away from blood vessels (where hormones are spawned), promoting spatial distribution through the TME. The bias strength is controlled by a \texttt{drift\_strength} parameter set to 0.25.
    \item \textbf{Simple diffusion.} The hormone selects a random move from a predefined set of directions biased toward positive $y$ and $z$ coordinates, or from the two axis-aligned directions $(0,0,1)$ and $(0,1,0)$. If the resulting position falls outside the grid, the hormone is removed from the simulation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Terminal Conditions}
\label{sec:md_terminal}
% ============================================================================

The simulation terminates when any of the following conditions is met. The \texttt{terminal\_condition()} method returns a tuple \texttt{(terminated, survival)} indicating whether the simulation should stop and whether the outcome represents patient survival (tumor elimination).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Maximum steps reached.} If $t \geq t_{\max}$ (\texttt{max\_steps}), the simulation terminates with \texttt{survival = False}. This represents the end of the observation period without definitive tumor clearance.

    \item \textbf{Tumor elimination.} If the tumor cell count drops to zero before reaching \texttt{max\_steps}, the simulation terminates with \texttt{survival = True}. This outcome indicates that the immune system (with or without treatment) successfully eliminated all tumor cells.

    \item \textbf{Tumor cell count exceeds threshold.} If the number of living tumor cells reaches or exceeds \texttt{max\_tumor\_cells} (default: 2000), the simulation terminates with \texttt{survival = False}. This condition represents uncontrolled tumor growth that has exceeded the model's capacity and, clinically, would correspond to a large, aggressive tumor mass.

    \item \textbf{Mean tumor growth rate exceeds threshold.} The mean tumor growth rate across all living tumor cells is computed as:
    \begin{equation}
        \bar{G} = \frac{1}{N_T} \sum_{i=1}^{N_T} \sigma\bigl(g_i + a_i,\; k=10\bigr)
        \label{eq:growth_rate}
    \end{equation}
    where $N_T$ is the tumor cell count, $g_i$ is the growth potential (combining \texttt{tumour\_growth\_effect}, DNA-derived proliferation chance, blood access, and angiogenesis), $a_i$ is the apoptosis-related component, and $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the sigmoid function with steepness $k = 10$. If $\bar{G} \geq w_{\text{tumour\_growth\_threshold}}$, the simulation terminates with \texttt{survival = False}. This condition detects cases where individual tumor growth rates are high even if the absolute cell count has not yet reached the threshold, capturing scenarios of rapidly accelerating tumor progression.
\end{enumerate}

These terminal conditions collectively ensure that simulations reach a definitive outcome---either tumor elimination or tumor dominance---within a bounded number of steps, while capturing both the absolute size and the growth dynamics of the tumor population.
