\chapter{Glucose Microenvironment System}
\label{ch:glucose}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=glucose!10, colframe=glucose!50, title=\textbf{The Invisible Battlefield}]
\textit{Cancer isn't just about rogue cells—it's about the metabolic war they wage for the most basic fuel of life: glucose. In this chapter, we reveal how a continuous chemical field shapes the discrete world of cellular combat, creating invisible barriers and hidden pathways that determine who lives and who dies.}
\end{tcolorbox}

Most cancer models focus on what you can see: cells dividing, immune cells attacking, tumors growing. But some of the most important action happens in the spaces between cells—in the invisible chemical landscape that guides cellular behavior and determines the outcome of immune-tumor interactions.

Our glucose microenvironment system represents one of the most innovative aspects of this model: a continuous 3D chemical field overlaid on our discrete agent grid, capturing the metabolic competition that shapes the tumor ecosystem. This isn't just computational sophistication for its own sake—it's biological necessity. Miss the glucose dynamics, and you miss a crucial mechanism of tumor survival and immune failure.

\section{Why Glucose Matters: The Metabolic Foundation of Cancer}
\label{sec:glucose-bio}

To understand why we need an explicit glucose field in our model, we need to appreciate the central role metabolism plays in cancer progression. This isn't just about cellular fuel—it's about evolutionary competition, spatial organization, and the fundamental constraints that shape which cells live and which die.

\subsection{The Warburg Effect: Cancer's Metabolic Rebellion}
\label{subsec:glucose-warburg}

In the 1920s, Otto Warburg made a discovery that seemed to defy logic \cite{warburg1956}. Cancer cells, even when surrounded by oxygen, preferred to metabolize glucose through the inefficient process of glycolysis rather than the much more efficient oxidative phosphorylation used by normal cells. Why would evolution favor such apparent waste?

The answer reveals cancer's strategic brilliance. The Warburg effect isn't a bug—it's a feature that provides multiple competitive advantages:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=tumorcell!10, colframe=tumorcell!50, title=\textbf{Cancer's Metabolic Strategy}]
\textbf{Speed over efficiency:} Glycolysis produces ATP faster than oxidative phosphorylation, even though it yields less energy per glucose molecule. For rapidly dividing cells, rate trumps efficiency.

\textbf{Building blocks for growth:} Glycolysis generates the biosynthetic precursors—nucleotides, amino acids, lipids—needed to construct new cancer cells.

\textbf{Environmental warfare:} Lactate production acidifies the local environment, creating conditions that favor tumor cells while impairing immune function.

\textbf{Independence from infrastructure:} Glycolysis doesn't require oxygen, allowing tumor cells to thrive even in poorly vascularized tissue regions.
\end{tcolorbox}

But this metabolic reprogramming comes with a cost: glucose addiction. Cancer cells consume glucose at rates up to 200 times higher than normal cells \cite{hanahan2011}, creating an intense competition for this limited resource.

\subsection{The Competition: Tumor Cells vs. Immune Cells}
\label{subsec:glucose-competition}

This is where the glucose field becomes crucial to our model. Tumor cells aren't competing for glucose in isolation—they're competing with the very immune cells trying to kill them. And in this metabolic arms race, proximity to glucose sources can mean the difference between life and death.

Activated immune cells, particularly T cells and NK cells, also depend heavily on glucose. When they encounter tumor antigens, they undergo their own metabolic switch to glycolysis, requiring substantial glucose for proliferation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic activity. But if tumor cells have already depleted the local glucose supply, these immune effectors arrive at the scene of battle already metabolically crippled.

\subsection{Spatial Consequences: Geography of Starvation}
\label{subsec:glucose-spatial}

This metabolic competition creates a spatial landscape of opportunity and limitation. Regions near blood vessels become glucose-rich zones where both tumor growth and immune function can thrive. Areas distant from vasculature, or surrounded by metabolically active tumor masses, become glucose deserts where immune cells lose their killing capacity not because they can't recognize their targets, but because they lack the metabolic fuel to eliminate them.

Our model captures this spatial heterogeneity explicitly, allowing regions of immune exclusion to emerge naturally from metabolic competition rather than being artificially programmed.

\section{The Mathematics of Metabolic Competition}
\label{sec:glucose-math}

Behind the biological drama lies mathematical precision. Our glucose field evolves according to the fundamental laws of diffusion and consumption—physics meets biology in a partial differential equation that captures how glucose spreads, concentrates, and disappears throughout the tumor microenvironment.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering

\caption{The glucose microenvironment system in action. Blood vessels inject glucose at discrete locations, which then diffuses throughout the 3D tissue according to Fick's laws while being consumed at dramatically different rates by tumor cells (Warburg effect) and immune cells (normal metabolism). The resulting glucose gradients create spatial patterns of metabolic competition—depletion zones around tumor masses where immune function is impaired, and glucose-rich regions near blood vessels where cellular competition is intense. Immune cells use these gradients for chemotaxis when no direct targets are available.}

\subsection{The Master Equation: Sources, Sinks, and Diffusion}
\label{subsec:glucose-pde}

The glucose concentration field $C(\mathbf{x}, t)$ evolves according to a reaction-diffusion equation that balances four fundamental processes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:glucose-pde}
\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} = D\,\nabla^2 C \;-\; \lambda\,C \;+\; S(\mathbf{x}) \;-\; Q(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}

This deceptively simple equation encodes a complex biological reality:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=bloodvessel!10, colframe=bloodvessel!50, title=\textbf{The Four Forces Shaping Glucose}]
\textbf{$D\,\nabla^2 C$}: Diffusion spreads glucose from high-concentration regions to low-concentration areas, following Fick's second law. Higher diffusion coefficients create more uniform glucose distributions.

\textbf{$-\lambda\,C$}: Natural decay represents the baseline metabolic consumption and glucose clearance that occurs even without active cellular uptake.

\textbf{$S(\mathbf{x})$}: Source terms inject fresh glucose at blood vessel locations, maintaining the supply lines that feed the tumor ecosystem.

\textbf{$-Q(\mathbf{x})$}: Consumption terms capture the dramatic differences in glucose uptake between tumor cells (Warburg effect) and immune cells (normal metabolism).
\end{tcolorbox}

The beauty of this formulation is that it lets spatial patterns emerge naturally. We don't have to program "glucose depletion zones"—they arise automatically when high-consumption tumor cells cluster together. We don't have to specify "metabolic gradients"—they form spontaneously as glucose diffuses away from blood vessel sources.

\subsection{Discrete 3D Laplacian}

The continuous Laplacian $\nabla^2 C$ is discretized on a regular 3D Cartesian grid using the standard 6-connected stencil.  For a grid point $(i, j, k)$ with unit spacing $\Delta x = 1$:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:laplacian-discrete}
\nabla^2 C(i,j,k) &= C(i{+}1,j,k) + C(i{-}1,j,k) \nonumber\\
                   &\quad + C(i,j{+}1,k) + C(i,j{-}1,k) \nonumber\\
                   &\quad + C(i,j,k{+}1) + C(i,j,k{-}1) \nonumber\\
                   &\quad - 6\,C(i,j,k).
\end{align}

This discrete Laplacian is implemented as a 3D convolution using \texttt{scipy.ndimage.convolve} with a $3 \times 3 \times 3$ kernel $K$ defined by:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kernel}
K[1,1,1] = -6, \qquad K[\text{face neighbors}] = 1, \qquad K[\text{all others}] = 0,
\end{equation}

\noindent where the six face-neighbor entries correspond to the positions
$(0,1,1)$, $(2,1,1)$, $(1,0,1)$, $(1,2,1)$, $(1,1,0)$, and $(1,1,2)$
in the kernel array.  All remaining 20 entries (edges and corners) are zero.

\subsection{Time Integration}

The diffusion equation is advanced in time using explicit (forward) Euler integration:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:euler}
C^{n+1}(i,j,k) = C^{n}(i,j,k) + D \cdot \nabla^2 C^{n}(i,j,k).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Stability Analysis}

By the von Neumann stability criterion for the explicit Euler scheme applied to the 3D diffusion equation on a unit grid, the stability condition is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stability}
D < \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.1\overline{6},
\end{equation}

\noindent which ensures that the amplification factor remains bounded.  The default value $D = 0.1$ satisfies this constraint.  After each diffusion step, the field is clamped to non-negative values to prevent unphysical negative concentrations.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions}

Neumann (zero-flux) boundary conditions are enforced by passing \texttt{mode='nearest'} to \texttt{scipy.ndimage.convolve}.  This mode replicates the boundary voxel values, effectively setting $\partial C / \partial \mathbf{n} = 0$ at all domain boundaries, which corresponds to no glucose flux through the walls of the simulation volume.

\section{Source Model}
\label{sec:glucose-source}

Glucose enters the simulation domain through blood vessel agents, which act as point sources.

\paragraph{Injection Mechanism.}
At each simulation tick, every \texttt{Blood} agent injects glucose at its grid position:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:injection}
C(\mathbf{x}_v) \leftarrow C(\mathbf{x}_v) + r_{\text{source}},
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{x}_v$ is the position of blood vessel agent $v$ and $r_{\text{source}}$ is the injection rate per step (\texttt{w\_glucose\_source\_rate}, default $5.0$).

\paragraph{Blood Vessel Geometry.}
Blood vessels are initialized along one edge of the simulation cube in a $4 \times 4$ cross-sectional arrangement (minus the four corner positions, yielding 12 vessel positions per slice along the $x$-axis).  This geometry creates a vascular channel that serves as the primary glucose supply for the entire domain.  Additional tumor-generated blood vessels (via angiogenesis) contribute further glucose sources during the simulation.

\section{Consumption Model}
\label{sec:glucose-consumption}

Glucose is consumed by both tumor cells and immune cells, with consumption handled individually during each agent's \texttt{step()} method.

\subsection{Tumor Cell Consumption}

Tumor cells consume glucose at a rate reflecting the Warburg effect:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:tumor-consumption}
C(\mathbf{x}_t) \leftarrow \max\!\bigl(0,\; C(\mathbf{x}_t) - r_{\text{tumor}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

\noindent where $r_{\text{tumor}} = \texttt{w\_glucose\_tumor\_consumption}$ (default $2.0$ per step).  The clamping to zero ensures that glucose concentrations remain non-negative.

\subsection{Immune Cell Consumption}

All immune cells (cytotoxic T cells, NK cells, macrophages, dendritic cells) consume glucose at a lower rate:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:immune-consumption}
C(\mathbf{x}_i) \leftarrow \max\!\bigl(0,\; C(\mathbf{x}_i) - r_{\text{immune}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

\noindent where $r_{\text{immune}} = \texttt{w\_glucose\_immune\_consumption}$ (default $0.5$ per step).

\subsection{Glucose-Dependent Growth Factor}

Tumor cell proliferation probability is modulated by local glucose availability:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:glucose-growth}
f_{\text{glucose}} = \min\!\bigl(1.0,\; C(\mathbf{x}_t) \cdot w_{\text{growth\_sensitivity}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

\noindent where $w_{\text{growth\_sensitivity}} = \texttt{w\_glucose\_growth\_sensitivity}$ (default $1.0$).  The duplication probability is then scaled by this factor:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p-duplication-glucose}
p_{\text{dup}}' = p_{\text{dup}} \cdot f_{\text{glucose}}.
\end{equation}

\noindent When glucose is abundant ($C \geq 1.0$), growth proceeds at the full rate.  As glucose drops below $1.0$, growth is proportionally suppressed.

\subsection{Glucose-Dependent Immune Function}

Immune cell kill rates are similarly modulated:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:glucose-immune}
f_{\text{immune}} = \min\!\bigl(1.0,\; C(\mathbf{x}_i) \cdot w_{\text{immune\_sensitivity}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

\noindent where $w_{\text{immune\_sensitivity}} = \texttt{w\_glucose\_immune\_sensitivity}$ (default $1.0$).  The effective kill probability becomes:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:p-kill-glucose}
p_{\text{kill}}' = p_{\text{kill}} \cdot f_{\text{immune}}.
\end{equation}

\noindent This mechanism captures the biological observation that T cells and NK cells require glucose to sustain their cytotoxic machinery.

\section{Gradient and Chemotaxis}
\label{sec:glucose-chemotaxis}

\subsection{Gradient Computation}

The glucose gradient at position $(i, j, k)$ is computed using central finite differences:

\begin{align}
\label{eq:gradient}
\frac{\partial C}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(i,j,k)} &= \frac{C(i{+}1,j,k) - C(i{-}1,j,k)}{2}, \nonumber\\[4pt]
\frac{\partial C}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(i,j,k)} &= \frac{C(i,j{+}1,k) - C(i,j{-}1,k)}{2}, \\[4pt]
\frac{\partial C}{\partial z}\bigg|_{(i,j,k)} &= \frac{C(i,j,k{+}1) - C(i,j,k{-}1)}{2}. \nonumber
\end{align}

\noindent At domain boundaries, the indices are clamped to the valid range (consistent with the Neumann boundary conditions), so the gradient naturally approaches zero near the walls.

\subsection{Discretized Gradient Step}

For agent movement on the integer grid, the continuous gradient is converted to a discrete step direction:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:discrete-step}
(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z) = \bigl(\mathrm{sign}(\nabla_x C),\; \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_y C),\; \mathrm{sign}(\nabla_z C)\bigr),
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathrm{sign}(v) \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$.  The resulting step is further clamped to ensure the target position lies within the grid bounds.

\subsection{Chemotaxis Behavior}

Immune cells that implement glucose chemotaxis---specifically \texttt{CytotoxicTCell}, \texttt{MacrophageM1}, \texttt{DendriticCell}, and \texttt{NaturalKiller}---employ the following fallback strategy:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item The agent first attempts to move directly towards a nearby tumor cell using the standard \texttt{move\_towards()} method.
  \item If no tumor target is found (i.e., \texttt{move\_towards()} returns \texttt{False}), the agent evaluates the glucose gradient at its current position.
  \item With probability $p_{\text{chemo}} = \texttt{w\_glucose\_chemotaxis\_strength}$ (default $0.3$), the agent moves one step in the direction of increasing glucose concentration.
  \item If the gradient is zero (flat concentration), no movement occurs.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent This mechanism provides immune cells with a biologically motivated navigation strategy: in the absence of direct target detection, cells move towards areas of higher metabolic activity, which correlates with the presence of functional vasculature and, consequently, viable tissue where tumor cells may reside.

\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:glucose-impl}

The glucose system is encapsulated in the \texttt{GlucoseField} class, located in \texttt{src/systems/glucose\_field.py}.

\subsection{Data Structure}

The glucose field is stored as a three-dimensional NumPy array of shape $(\texttt{width} \times \texttt{height} \times \texttt{depth})$ with \texttt{float64} precision, matching the dimensions of the agent grid.  The initial concentration is set uniformly to $1.0$.

\subsection{Convolution Kernel}

The 6-connected Laplacian kernel is constructed once during initialization as a $3 \times 3 \times 3$ NumPy array:

\begin{lstlisting}[caption={Laplacian kernel construction.},label={lst:kernel}]
self._kernel = np.zeros((3, 3, 3), dtype=np.float64)
self._kernel[1, 1, 0] = 1.0   # z-1
self._kernel[1, 1, 2] = 1.0   # z+1
self._kernel[1, 0, 1] = 1.0   # y-1
self._kernel[1, 2, 1] = 1.0   # y+1
self._kernel[0, 1, 1] = 1.0   # x-1
self._kernel[2, 1, 1] = 1.0   # x+1
self._kernel[1, 1, 1] = -6.0  # center
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Step Function}

The \texttt{step(model)} method executes the three phases of the glucose dynamics in sequence:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Injection:} iterate over all \texttt{Blood} agents and call \texttt{inject(pos, rate)} for each.
  \item \textbf{Diffusion:} apply the discrete Laplacian convolution and update the field via explicit Euler.
  \item \textbf{Decay:} multiply the entire field by $(1 - \lambda)$ where $\lambda$ is the decay rate.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Agent-level consumption (by tumor and immune cells) is handled within each agent's own \texttt{step()} method, not within the \texttt{GlucoseField.step()} call.  This design ensures that consumption occurs at the correct point in the agent execution order and avoids double-counting.

\subsection{Statistics Methods}

The \texttt{GlucoseField} class exposes four aggregate statistics, used by the data collection system to track metabolic dynamics over time:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \texttt{mean\_concentration()} -- mean glucose across all voxels,
  \item \texttt{total\_concentration()} -- total glucose in the domain,
  \item \texttt{min\_concentration()} -- minimum glucose (identifies depletion zones),
  \item \texttt{max\_concentration()} -- maximum glucose (identifies source neighborhoods).
\end{itemize}

\noindent These values are recorded at every simulation step and provide insight into the metabolic state of the microenvironment, including the formation and extent of glucose-deprived regions around the tumor mass.
