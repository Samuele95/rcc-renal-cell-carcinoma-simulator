\chapter{Theoretical Background}
\label{ch:theoretical_background}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=immunecell!10, colframe=immunecell!50, title=\textbf{Chapter Preview}]
\textit{Before we can build virtual tumors, we must understand the real biological systems they represent. This chapter constructs the theoretical foundation for everything that follows—from the mathematics of agent-based modeling to the intricate cellular choreography of cancer immunology.}
\end{tcolorbox}

Every computational model rests on theoretical foundations. Get these wrong, and even the most sophisticated simulation becomes an elaborate exercise in digital fiction. Get them right, and the model becomes a powerful lens for understanding complex biological reality.

This chapter builds that foundation systematically. We'll explore why agent-based modeling is uniquely suited to cancer biology, dive deep into the immunological battlefield where tumor and immune cells clash, examine the metabolic competition that shapes this struggle, and understand how modern cancer treatments attempt to tip the balance toward healing.

\section{Agent-Based Modeling: A Different Way to Think About Biology}
\label{sec:bg_abm}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{The Philosophy of Computational Emergence}
\label{subsec:bg_abm_formalism}

Most mathematical models of cancer start with the big picture—equations that describe how tumor populations grow, how immune responses develop, how treatments affect outcomes. This top-down approach has yielded important insights, but it faces a fundamental limitation: real biology is built from the bottom up.

Agent-based modeling flips this paradigm. Instead of writing equations for populations, we write rules for individuals. Instead of prescribing system behavior, we let it emerge from countless local interactions. It's the difference between composing a symphony and unleashing a jazz ensemble—both can create beautiful music, but only one captures the improvisational creativity that drives innovation.

An agent-based model consists of three essential elements, each reflecting a fundamental aspect of biological reality:

\textbf{Agents: The Individual Actors}

Every cell in our simulation is an autonomous entity with its own internal state, memory, and decision-making capability. A tumor cell doesn't just multiply according to a growth equation—it evaluates its local glucose levels, assesses immune threats, checks its DNA for growth-promoting mutations, and makes a probabilistic decision about whether to divide. Each agent carries its complete biological history: which mutations it has accumulated, what immune attacks it has survived, how much glucose it has consumed.

\textbf{Environment: The Shared Stage}

Our 3D spatial grid isn't just a computational convenience—it's a representation of tissue architecture. Cells interact with their immediate neighbors, creating gradients of signaling molecules, competing for space and resources. The glucose field overlays this discrete space with continuous chemical dynamics, because biology operates simultaneously at discrete (cellular) and continuous (molecular) scales.

\textbf{Rules: The Laws of Cellular Physics}

Each agent follows behavioral rules that encode biological knowledge: how T cells recognize targets, how tumor cells respond to DNA damage, how blood vessels form in response to growth signals. These rules are local (each agent acts based only on information in its neighborhood) and stochastic (reflecting the inherent randomness of biological processes).

The magic happens when these simple elements interact:

\begin{equation}
    s_i(t+1) = f\bigl(s_i(t),\, \mathcal{N}_i(t),\, E(t),\, \xi_i(t)\bigr)
    \label{eq:agent_update}
\end{equation}

where agent $i$'s state at time $t+1$ depends on its current state $s_i(t)$, its neighbors $\mathcal{N}_i(t)$, the local environment $E(t)$, and stochastic factors $\xi_i(t)$. The environment itself evolves based on all agent actions:

\begin{equation}
    E(t+1) = g\bigl(E(t),\, \{a_i(t)\}_{i=1}^{N}\bigr)
    \label{eq:env_update}
\end{equation}

This bidirectional coupling—agents affecting the environment, environment affecting agents—creates the complex feedback loops that characterize living systems.

\subsection{Why ABM Is Uniquely Suited to Cancer Biology}
\label{subsec:bg_abm_advantages}

Traditional mathematical approaches to cancer have yielded important insights, but they face inherent limitations when confronting the complexity of the tumor microenvironment. Agent-based modeling addresses these limitations directly:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=tumorcell!10, colframe=tumorcell!50, title=\textbf{Heterogeneity: Every Cell Tells a Story}]
In our model, each tumor cell carries a unique 16-gene DNA profile that determines its behavior. Some cells are aggressive growers thanks to VHL mutations, others are immune evasion specialists with high PD-L1 expression, still others are angiogenesis experts promoting blood vessel formation. This isn't computational overhead—it's biological reality. Tumor heterogeneity drives treatment resistance, and only by modeling it explicitly can we understand and potentially overcome it.
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Spatial Dynamics: Location Is Everything}

Cancer isn't a well-mixed soup of cells—it's a spatially structured ecosystem where neighborhood determines destiny. Tumor cells near blood vessels have access to glucose and grow rapidly. Immune cells at the tumor periphery face different challenges than those attempting to penetrate the tumor core. ABM captures these spatial phenomena naturally, allowing immune exclusion zones, angiogenic networks, and metabolic gradients to emerge from local cell-cell interactions.

\textbf{Stochasticity: Embracing Biological Noise}

Every biological process has an element of chance. Will this tumor cell divide successfully? Will this T cell recognize its target? Will this drug molecule reach its destination? Traditional deterministic models smooth over this randomness, missing the "what if" scenarios that can dramatically alter outcomes. ABM incorporates stochasticity at the individual agent level, generating the realistic variability observed in clinical practice.

\textbf{Modularity: Evolution by Design}

Biological systems are modular—new functionalities can be added without redesigning the entire system. ABM mirrors this modularity. Want to add a new immune cell type? Create a new agent class. Want to model drug resistance? Add resistance mechanisms to existing agents. Want to incorporate metabolic competition? Overlay a glucose field. This modularity has been crucial for the iterative development of our model, allowing us to add complexity gradually while maintaining a stable foundation.

\subsection{The Repast4Py Framework}
\label{subsec:bg_repast4py}

Repast4Py \cite{repast4py} is a Python-based agent-based modeling framework designed for large-scale distributed simulations. It extends the Repast HPC (High-Performance Computing) toolkit to the Python ecosystem, leveraging MPI (Message Passing Interface) \cite{mpi4py} for inter-process communication. The key abstractions provided by Repast4Py are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{\texttt{repast4py.core.Agent}.} The base class for all agents. Each agent is identified by a tuple \texttt{(id, type, rank)} where \texttt{id} is a locally unique integer, \texttt{type} is an integer agent type identifier, and \texttt{rank} is the MPI rank of the process that owns the agent.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{SharedContext}.} A distributed container for agents. The context manages agent ownership across MPI ranks and provides methods for adding, removing, and iterating over agents.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{SharedGrid}.} A distributed spatial projection that maps agents to discrete grid coordinates. Repast4Py supports multiple grid configurations:
    \begin{itemize}[nosep]
        \item \texttt{BorderType.Sticky}: agents that attempt to move beyond grid boundaries are clamped to the boundary (used in the present model).
        \item \texttt{OccupancyType.Multiple}: multiple agents can occupy the same grid cell (necessary for a dense microenvironment).
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Schedule runner.} The scheduling mechanism, initialized via \texttt{schedule.init\_schedule\_runner}, manages the execution order of model steps across MPI ranks, ensuring synchronization where required.
\end{itemize}

\label{subsec:bg_mesa_comparison}

The original RCC model was implemented in Mesa \cite{mesa}, a widely used Python ABM framework. Table~\ref{tab:mesa_vs_repast} summarizes the key differences between Mesa and Repast4Py that motivated the port.

The port to Repast4Py required significant restructuring. In Mesa, neighbor queries are handled by the grid object directly (e.g., \texttt{grid.get\_neighbors()}). In the Repast4Py implementation, a custom \texttt{spatial\_index} dictionary maps grid positions to agent lists, and utility functions in \texttt{grid\_utils} provide 3D neighborhood queries, empty-cell detection, and Moore neighborhood enumeration. Additionally, Repast4Py requires explicit \texttt{save()} methods on agents for MPI serialization, and the model manages agent lifecycle (creation, movement, removal) through centralized methods on the \texttt{RCCModel} class rather than through Mesa's scheduler.

\section{The Immune System's War Against Cancer: RCC Immunology Decoded}
\label{sec:bg_immunology}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Two Armies, One Mission}
\label{subsec:bg_innate_adaptive}

The battle against cancer unfolds on two fronts, each with its own strengths and tactical approach. Understanding this dual response is crucial for modeling how immunotherapies work—and why they sometimes fail.

\textbf{Innate Immunity: The Rapid Response Force}

Think of innate immunity as the body's equivalent of first responders—police, firefighters, and emergency medical technicians who arrive first at any crisis. These cells don't need detailed briefings or specialized training; they recognize danger through ancient pattern-recognition systems and respond immediately.

Natural killer (NK) cells patrol tissues looking for cells that have lost their "self" signals—the MHC class I molecules that mark healthy cells. When tumor cells downregulate these molecules to hide from T cells, they become visible to NK cells instead. It's a cellular game of whack-a-mole: hide from one immune arm, become vulnerable to another.

Macrophages come in two flavors that reflect the immune system's conflicted relationship with cancer. M1 macrophages are the warriors—they engulf tumor cells, present their fragments to other immune cells, and secrete inflammatory signals that rally the immune response. M2 macrophages tell a different story: originally evolved to heal wounds and resolve inflammation, they can be co-opted by tumors to promote growth, build blood vessels, and suppress immune responses.

Dendritic cells serve as the immune system's intelligence agency. They patrol tissues, sampling proteins from cells they encounter. When they find tumor-specific antigens, they race to nearby lymph nodes to brief T cells on what they've discovered. This antigen presentation is the crucial link between innate recognition and adaptive response.

\textbf{Adaptive Immunity: The Elite Special Forces}

While innate immunity provides rapid but non-specific responses, adaptive immunity brings precision and memory. T cells and B cells undergo extensive training in thymus and bone marrow, learning to distinguish friend from foe with exquisite specificity.

In kidney cancer, the interplay between innate and adaptive immunity determines whether the immune system can mount an effective response. Dendritic cells bridge the two arms, capturing tumor antigens and presenting them to naive T cells. But this chain of communication has multiple failure points: tumors can hide their antigens, suppress dendritic cell function, or corrupt the T cell response itself.

\subsection{T Cell Subtypes}
\label{subsec:bg_tcells}

The model distinguishes six T cell populations, reflecting the major functional categories relevant to anti-tumor immunity:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{CD8$^+$ Cytotoxic T Cells (CTLs).} The primary effectors of adaptive anti-tumor immunity. CTLs recognize tumor-specific antigens presented on MHC class I molecules and kill target cells through the release of perforin and granzymes. Their effectiveness is modulated by activation level, PD-1/PD-L1 checkpoint interactions, and local glucose availability.

    \item \textbf{CD8$^+$ Naive T Cells.} Antigen-inexperienced CD8$^+$ cells residing in the microenvironment. Upon encountering a dendritic cell presenting a matching neoantigen (determined by TCR-antigen Hamming distance), they differentiate into cytotoxic T cells.

    \item \textbf{CD4$^+$ Naive T Cells.} Precursors to helper and regulatory T cells. Depending on the local cytokine milieu and hormonal environment, they can differentiate into Th1, Th2, or Treg cells. Antigen presentation by dendritic cells or macrophages triggers differentiation into Th1 or Th2 with equal probability, conditional on TCR-antigen matching.

    \item \textbf{CD4$^+$ Th1 Helper T Cells.} Pro-inflammatory cells that produce IFN-$\gamma$ and promote anti-tumor immunity. In the model, Th1 cells recruit M1 macrophages and dendritic cells, promote tumor apoptosis, and can proliferate under the influence of accumulated Th1-proliferation effects. Their proliferation is modulated by sex hormones, with low estrogen favoring Th1 expansion and high estrogen suppressing it.

    \item \textbf{CD4$^+$ Th2 Helper T Cells.} Cells associated with a humoral immune response that produce IL-4, IL-5, and IL-13. In the tumor context, Th2 cells recruit M1 macrophages and cytotoxic T cells, but they also exert a mild pro-tumorigenic effect by increasing tumor growth. Th2 proliferation is enhanced by estrogen and progesterone, consistent with the shift toward Th2-dominant responses at high hormonal levels.

    \item \textbf{Regulatory T Cells (Tregs).} Immunosuppressive cells that play a central role in tumor immune evasion. Tregs inhibit cytotoxic T cell killing (reducing \texttt{t\_kill\_rate\_effect}), suppress T cell proliferation and activation, increase T cell apoptosis, and reduce dendritic cell phagocytosis. They differentiate from CD4$^+$ naive cells under the influence of the \texttt{treg\_differentiation\_effect}, which is promoted by high BMI and specific hormonal conditions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Antigen-Presenting Cells}
\label{subsec:bg_apcs}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dendritic Cells (DCs).} Professional antigen-presenting cells that form the critical link between innate and adaptive immunity. In the model, DCs migrate toward tumor cells, phagocytose them (with probability modulated by the \texttt{dc\_phagocitosis\_effect}), and then migrate toward naive T cells (CD4$^+$ or CD8$^+$, chosen randomly at creation) to present the captured antigens. A successful antigen presentation event triggers T cell activation---differentiation of a CD8$^+$ naive cell into a cytotoxic T cell or a CD4$^+$ naive cell into a Th1 or Th2 cell. When no tumor cells are in the neighborhood, DCs employ glucose chemotaxis to navigate toward nutrient-rich regions likely to harbor tumor activity.

    \item \textbf{Plasmacytoid Dendritic Cells (pDCs).} A specialized DC subtype with distinct functional properties. Like conventional DCs, pDCs phagocytose tumor cells and activate T cells. Additionally, pDCs can spawn new NK cells, providing a direct link between antigen presentation and innate cytotoxic capacity. pDCs also exert paracrine effects on their neighborhood: promoting angiogenesis, increasing Treg differentiation, modulating T cell proliferation, and enhancing both T cell and NK cell kill rates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Macrophages}
\label{subsec:bg_macrophages}

Tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs) exist along a polarization spectrum, with M1 and M2 representing the two extremes:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{M1 Macrophages (pro-inflammatory).} M1 macrophages are classically activated and exhibit anti-tumor properties. They phagocytose tumor cells, present antigens to CD4$^+$ naive T cells (similar to DCs), and exert local effects that increase T cell kill rates and promote Th1 proliferation. When no tumor target is available, M1 macrophages use glucose chemotaxis as a fallback navigation strategy. M1 macrophages can polarize to M2 under the influence of the \texttt{macrophage\_m1\_mutation\_effect}, which is enhanced by high BMI.

    \item \textbf{M2 Macrophages (anti-inflammatory / pro-tumorigenic).} Alternatively activated M2 macrophages promote tumor progression. They suppress T cell killing (negative \texttt{t\_kill\_rate\_effect}), increase tumor growth, and promote angiogenesis. M2 macrophages migrate toward tumor cells and can repolarize to M1 under appropriate conditions, although this transition is less likely than the M1-to-M2 shift in the tumor microenvironment.
\end{itemize}

The M1/M2 balance is a critical determinant of tumor outcome. A TME dominated by M1 macrophages favors immune-mediated tumor control, while M2 dominance promotes immune evasion and tumor progression. BMI influences this balance through the \texttt{macrophage\_m1\_mutation\_effect} and \texttt{macrophage\_m2\_mutation\_effect} fields in the effect system, reflecting clinical observations that obesity is associated with increased M2 polarization \cite{hanahan2011}.

\subsection{Natural Killer Cells}
\label{subsec:bg_nk}

Natural killer (NK) cells are innate lymphocytes capable of recognizing and killing tumor cells without prior antigen sensitization. NK cell cytotoxicity is governed by the balance between activating and inhibitory receptors on the NK cell surface. In particular, NK cells are inhibited by MHC class I molecules on target cells; tumors that downregulate MHC-I to evade CTL recognition become more susceptible to NK-mediated killing---a phenomenon known as the ``missing self'' hypothesis.

In the model, NK cell kill probability is determined by the \texttt{nkl\_kill\_rate\_effect}, which is influenced by BMI (higher BMI reduces NK effectiveness), neighboring immune cells, and local glucose concentration. NK cells also exert a positive effect on T cell kill rates in their neighborhood, providing paracrine support for adaptive immunity.

\subsection{Mast Cells and Neutrophils}
\label{subsec:bg_mast_neutrophils}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mast Cells.} These innate immune cells play a dual role in the TME. Depending on stochastic initialization, individual mast cells may promote or suppress tumor growth, enhance or inhibit T cell killing, promote angiogenesis, and modulate M1 macrophage recruitment. Mast cells can also spawn dendritic cells, amplifying the antigen presentation capacity of the TME. This functional heterogeneity---captured in the model by randomly assigning effect polarities at creation---reflects the biological observation that mast cells can be either pro- or anti-tumorigenic depending on context.

    \item \textbf{Neutrophils.} The most abundant innate immune cells in circulation. In the current model, neutrophils are present in the microenvironment but have minimal active behavior, serving primarily as a population marker. Future extensions may incorporate neutrophil-mediated tumor killing and NETosis.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Immune Checkpoints: The PD-1/PD-L1 Pathway}
\label{subsec:bg_checkpoints}

Immune checkpoints are inhibitory pathways that normally prevent autoimmunity but are co-opted by tumors to evade immune destruction. The PD-1/PD-L1 (programmed death-1 / programmed death-ligand 1) axis is the most clinically relevant checkpoint in RCC \cite{pardoll2012}.

PD-L1 (encoded by the \emph{CD274} gene) is expressed on tumor cells and binds to PD-1 on activated T cells, delivering an inhibitory signal that reduces T cell proliferation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic activity. In the model, when a cytotoxic T cell encounters a tumor cell, the tumor cell's \texttt{get\_PD1\_inhibition()} method determines whether PD-1/PD-L1 engagement occurs, based on the \emph{CD274} gene's mutation-expression score. If the checkpoint is engaged, the T cell's activation level is reduced rather than the tumor cell being killed.

\subsection{Tumor Immune Evasion Mechanisms}
\label{subsec:bg_evasion}

RCC tumors employ multiple strategies to evade immune destruction, several of which are captured in the model:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Antigen presentation loss.} Mutations in MHC, B2M, JAK1, JAK2, TAP1, or TAP2 genes reduce the probability of neoantigen presentation, making tumor cells invisible to T cells.
    \item \textbf{PD-L1 upregulation.} Overexpression of the \emph{CD274} gene product enables immune checkpoint engagement, suppressing T cell activity.
    \item \textbf{Treg recruitment.} The tumor microenvironment promotes differentiation of CD4$^+$ naive T cells into immunosuppressive Tregs.
    \item \textbf{M2 macrophage polarization.} Tumor-derived signals (represented through the effect system) promote M1-to-M2 repolarization, shifting the macrophage balance toward pro-tumorigenic phenotypes.
    \item \textbf{Metabolic competition.} Tumor cells consume glucose at elevated rates (Warburg effect), creating nutrient-depleted zones that impair immune cell function (see Section~\ref{sec:bg_metabolism}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{The Metabolic Battlefield: Why Glucose Matters in Cancer}
\label{sec:bg_metabolism}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{The Warburg Effect: Cancer's Metabolic Rebellion}
\label{subsec:bg_warburg}

In the 1920s, Otto Warburg made an observation that would fundamentally change how we understand cancer. He discovered that tumor cells, even when surrounded by oxygen, prefer to metabolize glucose through the inefficient process of glycolysis rather than the highly efficient oxidative phosphorylation used by normal cells \cite{warburg1956}.

This seemed crazy at first. Why would cancer cells choose a metabolic pathway that produces only 2 ATP molecules per glucose instead of the 36 ATP that normal cells generate? It was like choosing to walk everywhere when you have access to a Ferrari.

But cancer cells, it turns out, aren't optimizing for efficiency—they're optimizing for speed and flexibility. The Warburg effect provides several crucial advantages to rapidly growing tumors:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=glucose!10, colframe=glucose!50, title=\textbf{The Warburg Advantage: Fast and Flexible}]
\textbf{Speed:} Glycolysis produces ATP faster than oxidative phosphorylation, even though it produces less per glucose molecule. For rapidly dividing cells, rate matters more than efficiency.

\textbf{Building blocks:} Glycolysis generates the biosynthetic precursors needed to build new cells—nucleotides for DNA, amino acids for proteins, lipids for membranes.

\textbf{Acidification:} Lactate production acidifies the tumor microenvironment, impairing immune cell function and creating a hostile environment for tumor competitors.

\textbf{Oxygen independence:} Glycolysis doesn't require oxygen, allowing tumor cells to survive in the poorly oxygenated core of solid tumors.
\end{tcolorbox}

\centering
\input{figures/warburg_effect}
\caption{The Warburg effect in normal versus cancer cells. Normal cells efficiently process glucose through oxidative phosphorylation in mitochondria, generating 36 ATP molecules per glucose and requiring oxygen. Cancer cells opt for the "fast food" approach—glycolysis produces only 2 ATP per glucose but operates much faster and doesn't require oxygen. In our model, this metabolic reprogramming drives spatial dynamics throughout the tumor microenvironment. Tumor cells consume glucose at rates up to 200 times higher than normal cells (\texttt{w\_glucose\_tumor\_consumption}), creating zones of metabolic starvation around tumor masses. These glucose-depleted regions become immune deserts—areas where T cells and NK cells lose their killing capacity not because of immune evasion, but simply because they lack the metabolic fuel needed for their effector functions.

\subsection{Metabolic Competition in the TME}
\label{subsec:bg_metabolic_competition}

The metabolic demands of tumor cells place them in direct competition with immune cells for limited glucose. Activated T cells, in particular, undergo a metabolic switch upon activation, becoming heavily dependent on glycolysis for their effector functions. When glucose is scarce, T cell proliferation, cytokine production, and cytotoxic capacity are all impaired \cite{hanahan2011}.

This metabolic competition creates a positive feedback loop that favors the tumor: as tumor cells consume glucose, immune cells in the depleted zone become less effective, allowing further tumor expansion and greater glucose depletion. Breaking this cycle---for instance, through anti-angiogenic therapy that paradoxically normalizes vasculature and improves nutrient delivery, or through direct metabolic intervention---is an active area of therapeutic research.

\subsection{Glucose as a Limiting Factor for Immune Function}
\label{subsec:bg_glucose_immune}

In the model, glucose availability modulates immune cell behavior through two mechanisms:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Kill probability impairment.} The kill probability of cytotoxic T cells and NK cells is scaled by a glucose factor:
    \begin{equation}
        p_{\text{kill,effective}} = p_{\text{kill,base}} \times \min\bigl(1,\; C_{\text{glucose}}(\mathbf{x}) \cdot w_{\text{glucose\_immune\_sensitivity}}\bigr)
        \label{eq:glucose_impairment}
    \end{equation}
    where $C_{\text{glucose}}(\mathbf{x})$ is the local glucose concentration at position $\mathbf{x}$. When glucose is plentiful, the factor saturates at 1; when glucose is depleted, the kill probability is proportionally reduced.

    \item \textbf{Chemotaxis.} When immune cells (CTLs, M1 macrophages, DCs, NK cells) have no direct target in their neighborhood, they follow the local glucose gradient as a fallback navigation strategy:
    \begin{equation}
        \Delta\mathbf{x} = \text{sign}\bigl(\nabla C_{\text{glucose}}(\mathbf{x})\bigr)
        \label{eq:chemotaxis}
    \end{equation}
    This behavior reflects the biological observation that immune cells are attracted to metabolically active regions, and it provides a plausible mechanism for immune cell infiltration toward tumor masses that have not yet been directly detected.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Modern Cancer Treatment: Unleashing and Starving the Tumor}
\label{sec:bg_treatments}
% ============================================================================

\subsection{Immune Checkpoint Inhibitors: Removing the Brakes}
\label{subsec:bg_ici}

Imagine if your car's brake pedal was stuck, preventing you from driving even when you desperately needed to get somewhere. That's essentially what happens to T cells in the tumor microenvironment. Cancer has evolved sophisticated molecular "brake pedals"—immune checkpoints—that prevent T cells from attacking tumor cells, even when they recognize them as foreign.

The most important of these brakes in kidney cancer is the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway \cite{pardoll2012}. Tumor cells express PD-L1 (programmed death-ligand 1) on their surface like a molecular "don't shoot" sign. When this binds to PD-1 receptors on T cells, it delivers an inhibitory signal that essentially tells the T cell to stand down.

\textbf{Breaking the Molecular Handshake}

Immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) work by blocking this inhibitory handshake. Anti-PD-1 antibodies (like nivolumab and pembrolizumab) bind to the PD-1 receptor on T cells, while anti-PD-L1 antibodies (like avelumab) bind to PD-L1 on tumor cells. Either way, the result is the same: the brake is released, and T cells can resume their tumor-killing mission.

\subsection{Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitors: Starving the Beast}
\label{subsec:bg_tki}

While ICIs unleash the immune system, tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) take a different approach—they starve the tumor by cutting off its blood supply. Most kidney cancers begin with mutations in the VHL gene, which leads to overproduction of VEGF (vascular endothelial growth factor). VEGF acts like a "build more roads" signal, promoting the formation of new blood vessels that feed the growing tumor \cite{rini2019}.

TKIs work by blocking VEGF receptors, essentially putting up roadblocks that prevent new blood vessel formation. Without adequate blood supply, tumors struggle to grow and may even begin to shrink.

\subsection{The Power of Combination: 1 + 1 = 3}
\label{subsec:bg_combination}

Here's where cancer treatment gets interesting: combining ICI and TKI doesn't just add their effects—it multiplies them. This synergy has revolutionized kidney cancer treatment and forms the basis for current standard-of-care protocols \cite{rini2019, motzer2019}.

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=treatment!10, colframe=treatment!50, title=\textbf{The Synergy Cascade}]
\textbf{TKI sets the stage:} By blocking blood vessel formation, TKI reduces tumor burden and normalizes the chaotic tumor vasculature. Paradoxically, this creates better access routes for immune cells to infiltrate the tumor.

\textbf{TKI releases evidence:} When tumor cells die from TKI-induced starvation, they release their internal contents—including tumor-specific antigens that dendritic cells can capture and present to T cells.

\textbf{ICI removes the brakes:} Just as the immune system is being primed by TKI-released antigens, ICI removes the PD-1/PD-L1 brake, allowing newly activated T cells to launch a full assault on remaining tumor cells.

\textbf{Amplified response:} The result is an immune response that's both stronger and more sustained than either treatment alone could achieve.
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Implementation in Our Model}

Our computational framework captures these treatment mechanisms through multiple pathways:

For ICI treatment:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Direct PD-1/PD-L1 blockade prevents tumor-mediated T cell inhibition
    \item T cell reactivation restores the killing capacity of exhausted immune cells  
    \item Enhanced immune infiltration increases the number of tumor-fighting cells recruited to the site
\end{itemize}

For TKI treatment:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Growth inhibition directly reduces tumor cell proliferation rates
    \item Anti-angiogenesis blocks new blood vessel formation, limiting nutrient supply
    \item Neoantigen release from dying tumor cells primes adaptive immune responses
    \item Reduced Treg differentiation counteracts immunosuppressive mechanisms
\end{itemize}

\textbf{The ARON-1 Connection}

Our model goes beyond standard combination therapy to investigate a crucial clinical question: does the optimal treatment strategy depend on patient sex? The ARON-1 study \cite{santoni2024} provides real-world data showing that male and female patients respond differently to the same treatments—differences our model attributes to sex hormone effects on immune function.

By implementing four treatment configurations (none, ICI alone, TKI alone, and ICI+TKI combination) and coupling them with our comprehensive sex hormone system, we can explore whether personalized treatment selection based on biological sex could improve outcomes for kidney cancer patients.
