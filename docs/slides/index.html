Introduction & Motivation

Renal Cell Carcinoma — The Clinical Challenge

  • 3rd most common urological cancer
  • ~400,000 new cases per year worldwide
  • Metastatic 5-year survival <15%
  • Clear-cell RCC (ccRCC) accounts for ~75% of cases
  • Strong sex differences:
    • Incidence ratio ≈ 2:1 (male : female)
    • Females show better immunotherapy response

Renal cell carcinoma represents one of the most challenging cancers in oncology. The stark difference in survival rates and the pronounced sex differences make it an ideal candidate for computational modeling to understand underlying mechanisms.

Why Agent-Based Modeling?

Strengths of ABMs

  • Natural representation of heterogeneous cell populations
  • Capture spatial dynamics and local interactions
  • Emergent behaviour arises from simple rules
  • In silico experimentation — fast, ethical, cheap
  • Stochasticity matches biological variability

Limitations of ODE/PDE Models

  • Assume well-mixed populations
  • Difficult to capture cell-level heterogeneity
  • No spatial resolution
  • Hard to model discrete events (mutation, death)
Agent-based models provide a natural framework for modeling cancer as a complex adaptive system. Unlike traditional mathematical models, ABMs can capture the heterogeneity and spatial dynamics that are crucial in cancer biology.

Project Objectives

1. Port Mesa → Repast4Py: migrate the existing RCC ABM to a scalable MPI-based framework
2. Add glucose microenvironment: 3D reaction–diffusion field modelling the Warburg effect
3. Validate against clinical data: fit model to ARON-1 clinical survival outcomes
4. Bayesian optimisation: tune 114 parameters with Optuna (TPE sampler, 250 trials)
5. Sex-specific analysis: compare male vs. female immune dynamics under treatment
Our objectives span from technical implementation challenges to biological validation. The focus on sex-specific differences is particularly important given the clinical evidence of differential treatment responses.

Immune System Overview

Innate Immunity

  • M1 Macrophages — phagocytosis, pro-inflammatory
  • M2 Macrophages — tissue repair, pro-tumour
  • NK Cells — MHC-independent killing
  • Dendritic Cells (DC) — antigen presentation
  • Plasmacytoid DC — type I IFN production
  • Mast Cells — angiogenesis modulation
  • Neutrophils — early response

Adaptive Immunity

  • CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells — direct tumour killing
  • CD8+ Naïve T Cells — precursors
  • CD4+ Naïve T Cells — helper precursors
  • Th1 Cells — promote CTL & M1
  • Th2 Cells — promote humoral response
  • Treg Cells — immune suppression
Also modelled: Adipocytes, Blood vessels, Sex hormones, Cytokines
Our model captures the full complexity of both innate and adaptive immune responses. This comprehensive representation is crucial for understanding how immunotherapy treatments work at the cellular level.

The Warburg Effect

  • Tumour cells preferentially use aerobic glycolysis even in normoxia
  • Glucose consumption rate ~10–200× higher than normal cells
  • Creates metabolic competition with immune cells
  • Glucose-depleted microenvironment impairs:
    • T cell activation and proliferation
    • NK cell cytotoxicity
    • DC antigen presentation
  • Glucose availability becomes a limiting factor for immune function

The Warburg effect is central to our model. By explicitly modeling glucose as a limiting resource, we capture a key mechanism of tumor-immune interaction that is often overlooked in computational models.

3D Grid Environment

SharedGrid Configuration

  • SharedGrid — 3D, MPI-distributed
  • BorderType.Sticky — clamped boundaries
  • OccupancyType.Multiple — agents co-locate
  • Grid dimension: d = ⌊(V/B³)^(1/3)⌋

Spatial Index

  • dict[(x,y,z)] → list[Agent]
  • O(1) lookup, O((2r+1)³) neighbour scan
  • Moore (26-connected) or Von Neumann
  • Updated on every add/remove/move

Deferred Movement

  1. Agent sets _desired_pos in its step()
  2. All agents finish stepping
  3. model.move_all_agents() applies moves
  4. Avoids conflicts during iteration
The 3D grid environment is crucial for modeling spatial tumor growth and immune cell migration. The deferred movement pattern ensures computational stability while maintaining biological realism.

Simulation Loop — model.step()

Main Steps (1-5)

  1. Collect data — log counts, glucose stats
  2. Check termination — max steps, tumour eradicated, tumour cap, growth threshold
  3. Apply effects — neighbour-based effect collection
  4. Treatment — ICI / TKI / ICI+TKI (after treatment_start)
  5. Sex drift — CD8 population modulation

Main Steps (6-11)

  1. Glucose step — inject → diffuse → decay
  2. Angiogenesis — add/refresh blood vessels
  3. Update search dimension
  4. Shuffle & step all agents
  5. Deferred move — apply _desired_pos
  6. Spawn hormones — from blood vessels
Termination Conditions:
steps ≥ max_steps | tumour cells = 0 (survival) | tumour cells ≥ 2000 | mean growth rate ≥ threshold
The simulation loop orchestrates all model dynamics in a carefully sequenced manner. Each step represents a time unit where all biological processes are updated consistently.

Key Systems

DNA Mutation Model

Genome Structure

  • 16 genes encoded as nucleotide strings
  • Each gene: promoter (TATA box) + coding region + stop codon
  • Full codon table (64 codons → 20 amino acids + stop)
  • Wildtype protein = 20 amino acids per gene
  • Total DNA sequence ~3200 nucleotides

Mutation Mechanics

  • Point mutations during duplicate()
  • Mutation rate driven by genomic instability (BRCA1)
  • Number of mutations: n = ⌊r_mut × L⌋
  • Random nucleotide substitution (A, T, C, G)
  • Protein similarity via Levenshtein distance
  • Sex-dependent initial mutations: 3 (M) vs. 1 (F)
MHC, B2M, CD274, BRCA1, TP53, RB1, CDKN2A, MYC, RAS, EGFR, HER2, JAK1, JAK2, TAP1, TAP2, APC
The DNA mutation model provides mechanistic depth to tumor evolution. By modeling actual nucleotide sequences and codon translation, we capture realistic protein-level changes that affect cellular behavior.

Treatment System

ICI — Anti-PD-1/PD-L1

  • Blocks PD-1/PD-L1 axis on tumour cells
  • Restores T cell activation effect
  • Increases immune infiltration (Th1, Th2, Mast)
  • Effectiveness ∝ w_ici_effectiveness

Combination: ICI + TKI

  • Each drug applied with proportion = 1/N_drugs
  • Effects are proportionally averaged
  • Synergy: TKI releases neoantigens → ICI boosts T cell response

TKI — Anti-VEGF / Tyrosine Kinase

  • Reduces tumour growth effect: g ← g × (1 - e)
  • Reduces angiogenesis effect: a ← a × (1 - e)
  • Releases neoantigens (TKI_effect)
  • Reduces Treg differentiation
  • Effectiveness ∝ w_tki_effectiveness
Treatment starts at step treatment_start
(default: 100)
The treatment system models the two main classes of therapies used in RCC: immune checkpoint inhibitors and tyrosine kinase inhibitors. The combination approach reflects current clinical practice.

Glucose Field Implementation

Reaction–Diffusion Equation

∂C/∂t = D∇²C - λC + S(x) - Q(x)

  • C: glucose concentration
  • D: diffusion coefficient (< 1/6 for stability)
  • λ: natural decay rate
  • S(x): source term (blood vessels)
  • Q(x): consumption (tumour + immune)

Numerical Scheme

  • NumPy 3D array: np.float64
  • scipy.ndimage.convolve with 6-connected Laplacian kernel
  • Neumann (zero-flux) BCs via mode='nearest'
  • Stability: D < 1/6 ≈ 0.167

Step Sequence

  1. Blood vessels inject glucose
  2. Diffuse via discrete Laplacian
  3. Apply natural decay: C ← C(1-λ)
  4. Agents consume in their own step
The glucose field implementation uses standard numerical methods for reaction-diffusion equations. The explicit finite difference scheme is stable and computationally efficient.

Optuna Bayesian Optimisation

Setup

  • Sampler: Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE)
  • Trials: 250
  • Repeats: 1 per trial (stochastic seed)
  • Objective: minimise mean squared error vs. clinical data

Objective Function

L = (1/N) Σ { (stepsi - OSi)² if outcome matches
500² otherwise (high penalty) }

Clinical Data: ARON-1

  • Real-world patient cohort
  • Features: sex, BMI, treatment, concentrations
  • Target: overall survival (OS) in steps
  • Binary outcome: survival vs. death

Output

  • Best parameter set saved to JSON
  • Trial errors logged to CSV
  • Convergence curve: best error vs. trial number
Bayesian optimization provides an efficient approach to navigating the high-dimensional parameter space. The TPE algorithm is particularly well-suited to this type of optimization problem.

Empirical Study

Statistical Validation: Multi-Seed Design

Experimental Design

  • 160 simulations: 20 random seeds × 8 scenarios
  • 8 scenarios: 2 sexes (M/F) × 4 treatments (None, TKI, ICI, ICI+TKI)
  • Each run: up to 300 steps, tuned parameters for visible treatment effects
  • Why? Single-seed results can be misleading in stochastic ABMs

Statistical Methods

  • Survival rate: fraction of runs where tumour cells = 0
  • Mann-Whitney U test: non-parametric comparison of male vs. female outcomes
  • Reported: mean ± standard deviation across 20 seeds
The multi-seed design is crucial for validating stochastic models. What appears significant in a single run may disappear when averaged across multiple seeds.

  • Female ICI+TKI: PROGRESSION
  • 6 tumour cells remaining at step 299
  • Appears to be the worst combination scenario

Multi-Seed Result (n=20)

  • Female ICI+TKI: 65% SURVIVAL
  • Actually the best scenario overall
  • Consistent with clinical evidence
Lesson: Single-seed analysis of stochastic models can be misleading.
Statistical validation across multiple seeds is essential for reliable conclusions.
